About

Data, with a purpose.

Honestly, I got into data because I liked being the person who could answer "wait, is that actually true?" with evidence instead of a guess. That's still what I enjoy most.

I just finished my master's at George Mason University (Data Analytics Engineering, 3.97 GPA), and along the way I've worked on everything from a capstone wiring an AI assistant into a climate database, to predicting bridge conditions across 124,000+ records, to digging through Google Ads spend to figure out where the money was actually going.

I'm comfortable across SQL, Python, Tableau, and Power BI, but the tool matters less than the habit: clean the data first, question the result, and explain it in a way that doesn't need a glossary. I care a lot about getting the small things right — a wrong join or an unhandled null can quietly break an entire analysis.

Recipe Reviews Sentiment & Fake-Review Detection

Recipe reviews are messy and emotional, so I ran them through VADER and TextBlob for sentiment, used LDA to find what people kept talking about, and then trained models to flag reviews that didn't add up — a One-Class SVM first, then XGBoost with SMOTE once I had labeled examples. About 90% of reviews were positive; roughly 10% looked off.

Garment Worker Productivity Analysis

I wanted to know what actually drives garment-worker productivity, so I compared seven regression models to predict it and used logistic regression to flag underperformers early. The interesting part was SHAP and LIME — they showed overtime, idle time, and work-in-progress were the real levers, which is something an HR manager can act on.

I looked at 124,988 bridges across all 52 U.S. states and territories to see how condition relates to climate stress. After cleaning the data, I pulled together deck ratings, bridge age, materials, and weather factors like snowfall and freeze-thaw cycles into Power BI dashboards — the goal being to spot which bridges are aging worst and where the climate is hardest on them.
